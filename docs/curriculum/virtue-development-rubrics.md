# Virtue Development Rubrics and Indicators

A framework for intentional character formation through observable indicators aligned with classical and Christian virtue traditions.

---

## Philosophy

### Why Virtue Education?

1. **Integral to classical education**: The goal is not just knowledge but wisdom and goodness
2. **Character is taught**: Virtue requires modeling, practice, and habit formation
3. **Academic and moral unity**: Knowledge without virtue is dangerous; virtue without knowledge is blind
4. **Community formation**: Shared virtues create school culture
5. **Lifetime preparation**: Character matters more than credentials

### Approach

**Not moralism**: We do not reduce virtue to rule-following or external compliance.

**Not relativism**: We affirm objective moral truth while acknowledging developmental growth.

**Growth-oriented**: We measure progress, not perfection; effort, not arrival.

**Grace-informed**: We recognize human weakness while calling students higher.

## Core Virtues

### Classical Cardinal Virtues

| Virtue | Definition | Opposite |
|--------|------------|----------|
| **Prudence** | Right reason in action; practical wisdom | Foolishness, Rashness |
| **Justice** | Giving to each what is due | Injustice, Partiality |
| **Fortitude** | Courage and perseverance in difficulty | Cowardice, Rashness |
| **Temperance** | Moderation and self-control | Excess, Deficiency |

### Christian Theological Virtues

| Virtue | Definition | Opposite |
|--------|------------|----------|
| **Faith** | Trust in God and His revelation | Unbelief, Presumption |
| **Hope** | Confident expectation of God's promises | Despair, Presumption |
| **Love** | Willing the good of others; charity | Selfishness, Indifference |

### Supporting Virtues

| Virtue | Related To | Definition |
|--------|------------|------------|
| **Humility** | Temperance | Accurate self-assessment |
| **Diligence** | Fortitude | Careful, persistent effort |
| **Honesty** | Justice | Truthfulness in word and deed |
| **Respect** | Justice | Honoring dignity of others |
| **Gratitude** | Justice/Love | Thankfulness for gifts received |
| **Generosity** | Love/Justice | Giving freely to others |

## Developmental Progression

### Grammar Stage (Grades K-4): Formation

**Focus**: Building habits through routines, stories, and imitation

**Characteristics**:
- External motivation initially (praise, consequences)
- Concrete understanding of right and wrong
- Learning through stories and heroes
- Habit formation through repetition
- Beginning self-awareness

**Approach**:
- Consistent routines and expectations
- Stories of virtue and vice
- Concrete examples and role models
- Simple, clear language about virtues
- Immediate feedback
- Celebrate effort and growth

### Logic Stage (Grades 5-8): Understanding

**Focus**: Understanding reasons for virtue; connecting actions to principles

**Characteristics**:
- Growing internal motivation
- Understanding "why" behind rules
- Analyzing virtue in literature and history
- Recognizing complexity and nuance
- Developing conscience

**Approach**:
- Explain reasons and principles
- Analyze characters and decisions
- Discuss ethical dilemmas
- Connect virtues to subjects
- Socratic questioning
- Peer accountability

### Rhetoric Stage (Grades 9-12): Ownership

**Focus**: Personal conviction; virtue as identity; influence on others

**Characteristics**:
- Internal motivation
- Articulate defense of values
- Complex ethical reasoning
- Leadership and modeling
- Lifelong commitment forming

**Approach**:
- Philosophical ethics study
- Leadership opportunities
- Mentoring younger students
- Service and application
- Personal reflection and goals
- Accountability relationships

## Virtue Rubrics by Stage

### Grammar Stage (K-4)

#### Prudence

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|------------|
| **Emerging** | With prompting, stops to think before acting; sometimes considers consequences |
| **Developing** | Often pauses to think; can identify likely results of actions |
| **Proficient** | Usually thinks before acting; asks for help when unsure; makes reasonable choices |
| **Exemplary** | Consistently makes thoughtful choices; helps others think through decisions |

#### Justice

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|------------|
| **Emerging** | With reminders, shares and takes turns; sometimes treats others fairly |
| **Developing** | Usually shares and takes turns; recognizes unfairness when pointed out |
| **Proficient** | Treats others fairly; speaks up when sees unfairness; keeps promises |
| **Exemplary** | Goes out of way to be fair; includes others; is trustworthy |

#### Fortitude

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|------------|
| **Emerging** | Attempts difficult tasks with encouragement; sometimes perseveres |
| **Developing** | Tries again after failure with support; beginning to face fears |
| **Proficient** | Perseveres through difficulty; tries new things despite fear; doesn't give up easily |
| **Exemplary** | Embraces challenges; helps others persevere; remains calm in difficulty |

#### Temperance

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|------------|
| **Emerging** | With reminders, waits turn and controls impulses; sometimes patient |
| **Developing** | Usually waits appropriately; can calm down with guidance |
| **Proficient** | Controls impulses; patient with others; moderate in habits |
| **Exemplary** | Self-controlled; helps others calm down; content with enough |

#### Honesty

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|------------|
| **Emerging** | Tells truth when directly asked; sometimes admits mistakes |
| **Developing** | Usually truthful; beginning to own mistakes; doesn't cheat |
| **Proficient** | Truthful even when difficult; admits mistakes; doesn't exaggerate |
| **Exemplary** | Consistently honest; integrity even when not watched; values truth |

#### Kindness

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|------------|
| **Emerging** | Kind when prompted; sometimes notices others' feelings |
| **Developing** | Often kind without prompting; notices when others need help |
| **Proficient** | Consistently kind; helps without being asked; considers others' feelings |
| **Exemplary** | Goes out of way for others; includes outsiders; kind even to unkind |

#### Respect

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|------------|
| **Emerging** | With reminders, listens to adults; sometimes polite |
| **Developing** | Usually respectful to adults and peers; learning politeness |
| **Proficient** | Respectful in speech and action; honors authority; treats everyone with dignity |
| **Exemplary** | Models respect; respectful even when disagrees; honors everyone's dignity |

#### Diligence

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|------------|
| **Emerging** | Completes work with reminders; sometimes careful |
| **Developing** | Usually finishes work; trying to improve quality |
| **Proficient** | Completes work carefully; checks work; uses time well |
| **Exemplary** | Takes pride in excellent work; self-motivated; helps others |

### Virtue Through Content Areas

#### Mathematics
- **Prudence**: Logical reasoning, careful calculation
- **Diligence**: Persistence through difficult problems
- **Honesty**: Integrity in work, not copying
- **Temperance**: Patience with process

#### Science
- **Prudence**: Scientific method, evidence-based reasoning
- **Honesty**: Accurate data, intellectual integrity
- **Humility**: Recognizing limits of knowledge
- **Justice**: Ethical use of technology

#### History
- **Justice**: Evaluating historical figures and events
- **Prudence**: Learning from past wisdom and folly
- **Fortitude**: Heroes who showed courage
- **All virtues**: Character analysis in historical figures

#### Literature
- **All virtues**: Character analysis and evaluation
- **Prudence**: Understanding moral complexity
- **Love**: Empathy for characters
- **Justice**: Moral evaluation

#### Grammar/Writing
- **Diligence**: Careful, excellent work
- **Honesty**: Authentic voice, original work
- **Humility**: Accepting criticism and revision
- **Respect**: Appropriate language

#### Bible
- **All virtues**: Direct instruction and modeling
- **Faith, Hope, Love**: Theological virtues
- **Humility**: Recognizing need for grace

## Assessment Approach

### Principles

1. **Growth over perfection**: We measure progress, not arrival
2. **Process over outcome**: We value effort and intention
3. **Narrative over numbers**: Stories capture character better than grades
4. **Observation over testing**: Virtue is lived, not examined
5. **Encouragement over judgment**: We call students higher, not shame them

### Methods

#### Teacher Observation
- Daily interactions
- Classroom behavior
- Academic integrity
- Social situations
- Response to difficulty

**Documentation**:
- Anecdotal notes
- Incident records (positive and concerning)
- Pattern identification

#### Self-Assessment
- Reflection journals
- Goal-setting
- Progress evaluation
- Confession and commitment

**Sample Prompts**:
- "When did you show fortitude this week? When did you give up too easily?"
- "How did you demonstrate kindness? When were you unkind?"
- "What virtue do you want to grow in this quarter?"

#### Peer Input
- Anonymous encouragement
- Team evaluations
- Conflict resolution records

#### Parent Input
- Home behavior
- Character outside school
- Growth noticed

### Reporting

#### Elementary Report (Quarterly)

For each virtue area:
- **Strength observed**: Specific example
- **Growth area**: What to work on
- **Encouragement**: Affirmation

Example:
> **Fortitude**: Sarah persevered through her first long-division unit, asking for help when stuck but not giving up. She can grow in taking risks in class discussionâ€”she often knows the answer but hesitates. Keep encouraging her to share her good thinking!

#### Middle School Report (Semester)

- Virtue self-assessment
- Teacher observations
- Specific goals
- Growth narrative

#### High School Report (Semester)

- Extended self-reflection
- Multiple teacher input
- Community service evaluation
- Leadership assessment
- Personal virtue goals

### Not Used

- Numerical grades for character
- Rankings or comparisons
- Public identification of struggles
- Punitive assessment

## Integration Structures

### Daily

- **Morning meeting**: Virtue focus, stories, goals
- **Class discussion**: Connect content to virtue
- **End of day**: Reflection

### Weekly

- **Virtue focus**: One virtue highlighted all week
- **Recognition**: Specific virtue sightings
- **Community meeting**: Discuss virtue in action

### Quarterly

- **Virtue reports**: Written narrative
- **Conferences**: Parents, student, teacher
- **Goals**: Set next quarter's focus

### Annual

- **Growth portfolio**: Examples from the year
- **Year-end reflection**: Major growth areas
- **Awards**: Recognize character (not just grades)

## Classroom Practices

### Morning Meetings

1. Greeting (practice respect, kindness)
2. Sharing (practice listening, empathy)
3. Activity (practice cooperation, fairness)
4. Morning message (virtue focus for day)

### Class Discussion

- Socratic seminars on ethical questions
- Character analysis in literature/history
- Ethical dilemmas in science
- Real-life application discussions

### Routines and Procedures

- Consistent expectations (builds temperance)
- Consequences tied to virtues
- Restoration over punishment
- Community repair after conflict

### Recognition

- "Caught being virtuous" notes
- Specific, non-comparative praise
- Share virtue sightings
- Avoid rewards that undermine intrinsic motivation
